SOXL Execution Monitor — Methodology

Reconciled Plan v1.0 · last revised 2026-07-15

§0 The System in Ten Lines

  1. SOXL is a 3x levered SOXX fund; volatility decay makes size the primary risk lever, not direction.
  2. Four sizing rules score realized 20-day volatility (rv20) against fixed and rolling thresholds; their average is the ensemble.
  3. A trend gate (SOXX vs its 200-day MA) scales that ensemble down when the long-term trend breaks.
  4. E_target = ensemble × gate is the one number this system exists to produce.
  5. Capital deploys in three tranches, not all at once, each with its own arming condition.
  6. A 10-point churn band stops the system from trading noise.
  7. Hard per-tranche and sleeve-level stop-outs exist independently of the sizing math, for survival.
  8. Implied-vs-realized vol (VRP) tells you whether options are cheap or rich, never whether to size up SOXL.
  9. Four tripwires can invalidate the entire base case; any one trip halts the plan for review.
  10. A sibling dashboard (SOXX Regime Monitor) supplies context signals — it never talks to this engine.

§1 Origin & Relationship to the SOXX Regime Monitor

This project began as the execution half of a plan whose research half already existed: soxx-regime-monitor watches SOXX for signs of institutional distribution using an overnight-vs-intraday return decomposition. That project answers "is something changing under the surface." This project answers a completely different question: "given the volatility and trend that already happened, how much SOXL should be held right now, and how does that translate into an actual ledger of fills."

The two are deliberately kept as separate dashboards with separate data, separate equity curves, and separate git histories. The engine decides, the monitor informs. Collapsing them into one system would let a discretionary read of the regime monitor quietly override a mechanical sizing rule — exactly the failure mode systematic position sizing is meant to prevent. Practically: nothing computed on this dashboard is allowed to read from the regime monitor's feed (enforced by a static test on the sizing engine's source code); the regime monitor's numbers appear here only as a captioned, visually subordinate panel.

§2 Derived Series & Definitions

All series below are computed on SOXX closes unless noted; SOXL is used only for the execution ledger, hard-cap P&L, and the attribution backtest — never for sizing.

ret_t = close_t / close_(t-1) − 1
Daily simple return.
rv20 = std(ret, 20) × √252
20-session realized volatility, annualized, sample standard deviation (ddof=1).
rv20_p90
Rolling 252-session 90th percentile of rv20, minimum 120 periods of history before it activates.
ma20 / ma50 / ma200
Simple moving averages of close, used for trend context and the trend gate.
id = close/open − 1, id20 = rolling 20-session sum of id
Intraday-return decomposition, used only for the T2 tranche trigger and dashboard display — computed independently from the regime monitor's own id20.

§3 The Ensemble Sizing Rules

Four rules each map rv20 to a target exposure fraction in [0, 1]. Two use the rolling 90th-percentile of realized vol as their trigger (adaptive to the current regime); two use fixed thresholds (a hard floor that doesn't drift with a slowly-rising vol regime). Averaging four independently-triggered rules smooths the size path instead of producing a single abrupt step function.

RuleTriggerVol budgetFormula
R1rv20 > p90(rv20)0.40min(1, 0.40/rv20)
R2rv20 > p90(rv20)0.50min(1, 0.50/rv20)
R3rv20 > 0.600.50min(1, 0.50/rv20)
R4rv20 > 0.550.40min(1, 0.40/rv20)

ENSEMBLE = (R1+R2+R3+R4) / 4.

Why volatility, not price, drives size: a 3x fund's daily rebalancing means variance drag scales with roughly 3σ² per day — the cost of holding leverage compounds with the square of realized vol, not with direction. A 40-vol regime and an 80-vol regime can carry the same trend and the same fundamental thesis, but the second costs roughly four times as much in pure decay to hold at the same size. De-levering is therefore triggered by how violent the tape is, not by whether it's up or down — hence four vol-only rules and a completely separate trend gate for direction.

Only extreme vol de-levers: at rv20 = 0.50 (a fairly ordinary semiconductor vol print) all four rules still return full exposure, since 0.50 sits under every fixed threshold and (typically) under the rolling p90. The rules only start cutting size once realized vol pushes into the tail of its own recent distribution or past the fixed 55-60% floors.

§4 Trend Gate & Tripwires

The trend gate T scales the ensemble by the long-term trend state of SOXX:

  • T = 1.0 while the latest close is above its 200-day MA.
  • T = 0.5 on any single close at or below the 200-day MA.
  • T = 0.0 after 5 consecutive closes at or below the 200-day MA — the counter resets to zero on any close back above it. T = 0.0 is sticky (stays zero every day the streak continues) and simultaneously raises tripwire #4 below.

E_target = round(ENSEMBLE × T, 3).

The four tripwires

Any one of these trips a dashboard-wide red banner reading "methodology review required; base case invalidated." Three are manually-flagged judgment calls (set in data/manual.json); one is computed automatically.

  1. ≥2 hyperscaler capex plateau — manual. The base case assumes continued AI-datacenter capex growth across at least three of the major hyperscalers; two or more flattening simultaneously would remove the demand thesis for semiconductor capacity.
  2. Delivered Fed hike — manual. The plan is sized for a cutting or hold-steady rate regime; an actual delivered hike (not just hawkish talk) changes the liquidity backdrop leveraged equity exposure depends on.
  3. TrendForce memory rollover — manual. A confirmed rollover in TrendForce's memory pricing data would signal the current up-cycle in memory (a major SOXX component) has turned.
  4. SOXX 5 consecutive closes below MA200 — computed automatically; identical condition to the gate's T = 0.0 state.

§5 Tranche Re-Deployment Plan

Capital deploys into SOXL in three tranches rather than all at once, so that entry timing itself carries some risk control independent of the sizing math:

  • T1 (25% of C) — discretionary event-branch entry (originally gated on the TSMC print); reported DEPLOYED once a fill is recorded in position.json, otherwise WAITING.
  • T2 (+20% of C) — arms when rv20 < 0.65 OR id20 > −0.03 (either condition, using this dashboard's own id20, never the regime monitor's).
  • T3 (up to E_target) — arms only when a manual "gauntlet cleared" flag is set, the trend gate is at least half-open (T ≥ 0.5), and no tripwire has tripped. This is the tranche that ultimately hands control to the mechanical target.

Once all three tranches show DEPLOYED, the tranche board collapses to a single "ENGINE GOVERNS" badge — from that point forward, §3.4's E_target/deployed gap alone drives every ACT/HOLD decision, with no further tranche-level gating.

§6 Hard Caps & Survival Sizing

The sizing math above optimizes for long-run compounding; it says nothing about surviving a single catastrophic gap. Two independent, always-on stop-outs exist purely for capital preservation, checked against each deployed tranche's actual cost basis:

  • Per-tranche cap: any single deployed tranche down 25% or more from its cost basis (boundary inclusive) is flagged BREACH-HALVE.
  • Sleeve cap: the capital-weighted blended P&L across all deployed tranches down 35% or more is flagged BREACH-FLAT and overrides everything else on the dashboard — a full red banner plus a GitHub issue every day the breach persists.
The June-5 gap lesson: no daily-close-based system, this one included, can prevent an overnight gap. Every rule in this document evaluates settled closes; a 3x fund can move 25%+ between one settled close and the next print with zero opportunity to react intraday. The hard caps exist to bound the damage after the fact, not to prevent it.

§7 VRP Playbook

VRP = IV30 − rv20 (IV30 is a manually-entered 30-day implied vol reading, dated and flagged stale after 7 sessions without an update).

  • VRP ≤ −0.10 → BUY OPTIONALITY: implied vol is cheap relative to what's actually realizing. Calls or puts are priced favorably here.
  • VRP ≥ +0.10 → DE-RISK WITH SHARES: insurance is rich; reducing share exposure directly is more efficient than buying expensive puts.
  • Otherwise → NEUTRAL.
Sign convention warning: a negative VRP zone means options are cheap to buy. It is never, under any zone, a signal to sell options — this plan carries no short-vol exposure and the VRP panel does not size SOXL itself.

§8 Execution Rules

  • MOC only. When ACT fires, the instruction is always to rebalance to target at the closing auction — never at the open, where spreads and gap risk on a 3x product are materially worse.
  • Preview run. A 3:50pm ET pipeline run (preview.json) surfaces the ACT/HOLD call on the live, not-yet-settled candle, specifically to inform the MOC decision minutes before the close. It never touches the settled signals.json ledger and never opens alert issues.
  • Churn band. ACT requires |E_target − deployed| ≥ 0.10 (10 points of C) — small drifts inside that band are ignored so the plan isn't rebalancing every session on sizing noise.
  • No-add windows. Events flagged in the calendar carry a 24-hour no-add window; the plan does not initiate new size directly ahead of a known binary catalyst.

§9 Known Failure Modes & Honesty Appendix

All four rule thresholds and both gate parameters were chosen with 2026 market behavior already in the training sample — this is in-sample parameter selection, and the backtest numbers in §3 should be read with that firmly in mind. A split-half stability check (fitting on the first half of the backtest window and evaluating on the second) produced a correlation of only ρ = 0.23 between the two halves' optimal parameters — a weak result that argues for treating the specific numeric thresholds as reasonable, not optimal, and revisiting them periodically rather than trusting a one-time fit.

A monthly stability audit is planned: re-run the split-half check on trailing data and flag if the live parameters have drifted meaningfully out of the stable region.

What would tear this plan up: any of the four tripwires in §4; a semiconductor-cycle regime change that invalidates the vol/trend relationship the ensemble rules assume (e.g. a structurally higher vol floor); or a persistent divergence between SOXX and the broader thesis it's meant to represent. Any of these should trigger a full methodology review, not a parameter tweak.
